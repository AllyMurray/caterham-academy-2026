Prep sequence

Start With The Factory Car, Then Prep For Racing

The useful way to think about Academy prep is not "what can I change?" but "when should this decision be made?" Some choices are easier during the build, some are post-handover race-prep jobs, and some are simply spares or setup parts to have ready before testing.

1

Understand The Handover Spec

Caterham handles IVA at the factory, so the car should arrive as a road-compliant car. Treat that as the baseline. Race prep starts after handover, where the aim is to make permitted competition changes without confusing them with road-registration requirements.

  • Keep the factory IVA handover spec separate from competition-only changes.
  • Anything that removes road-compliance equipment should be treated as race prep.
  • Use the full parts reference when you need to check a specific part number.

2

Make The Build-Time Decisions Early

The highest-value early decisions are the ones that affect bodywork, fuel-tank layout, cockpit fit, protection, or parts that are easier to install while the car is being built.

  • Choose between the standard aluminium tank route and the moulded FIA-FT3 fuel cell route.
  • Decide whether you want the passenger side impact protection bar before trim and hood expectations are set.
  • Discuss heat insulation, sill protectors, sidescreen choice, and driver's foot position during build planning.
  • Confirm the mandatory cooling and induction grille fitments are included in the Academy spec.

3

Convert From Road Handover To Race Spec

After handover, the job is to make the competition changes that only make sense once the car no longer needs to remain in its road-handover form. The clearest example is the race master cylinder cap: it is permitted, but it removes the low-fluid warning.

  • Fit the race master cylinder cap only as a post-handover race-prep change.
  • Plan any light removal, blanking covers, trim removal, or event-only bodywork changes as competition setup.
  • Confirm any immobiliser, switch, or fuel-pump wiring changes with Caterham or an appointed Caterham Motorsport Service Centre where required.

4

Prepare For Shakedown And Setup

Before testing, focus on the parts that affect feel, reliability, and learning. You want the driver position, brakes, data, and basic setup plan sorted before you start burning test time.

  • Have permitted competition front and rear brake pads ready before the first proper test.
  • Plan data, shift-light, camera, and dashboard layout early so wiring stays tidy.
  • Confirm seat, harness, shoulder-strap slots, pedal position, steering wheel spacing, and FHR suitability.
  • Gather permitted setup parts before geometry, ride-height, and ARB work begins.

5

Make It Event-Ready

For the first event, prioritise mandatory safety items and simple spares that keep a weekend alive. The full parts list is useful here, but the practical aim is scrutineering readiness and repairability.

  • Fit front and rear towing straps and the high intensity rear warning light with the correct clamp bracket.
  • Use permitted Toyo Proxes CF2 tyres sourced through HP Tyre Ltd, Daventry.
  • Carry common permitted bodywork spares: nylon rear wing screws, wingstay options, light blanking parts, and tape for temporary event repairs.
  • Avoid the specifically prohibited quicker ratio steering rack, part number 30S030A.

Part Number Rule

  • Caterham may change part numbers during the life of the regulations.
  • A later suffix that fully supersedes an earlier suffix may be used where the earlier suffix is listed.
  • If a standard part is not identified in the regulations by a specific part number, the standard part is the part number in the car's bill of materials or a later bill of materials for an identical specification car.
