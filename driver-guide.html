Your Responsibilities

The Academy is built around equal cars, tightly controlled rules, and novice drivers. The documents are clear that preparation and compliance are the driver's responsibility.

What That Means

  • Read the championship regulations, final instructions, and relevant NCR chapters before events.
  • If a modification is not clearly permitted, assume it is not permitted.
  • No professional team support is allowed at events; Caterham support is for advice and emergency help, not routine car preparation.
  • Safety scrutineering checks that the car and kit are safe. Eligibility scrutineering checks that the car matches the regulations.
  • The driver is ultimately responsible if the car is found non-compliant.
  • Use the Delivery prep, Race prep, and Spanner checks pages as operational checklists.

Language

Key Terms

These are the terms the Driver Guide expects you to understand before race weekends start moving quickly.

Term What it means
Assembly / collecting area Where cars gather before qualifying or a race. It is rarely the pit lane, so identify it from the final instructions.
Clerk of the Course The event decision maker. You normally see the Clerk at briefings or if an incident needs discussion.
FIA / Motorsport UK / NCR The FIA is the world governing body, Motorsport UK is the UK authority, and the National Competition Rules are the general UK motorsport rules. NCR references use chapter, appendix, and article numbers.
Final instructions Event-specific instructions covering timings, locations, briefings, passes, assembly, and procedures. Read them carefully.
FHR / HANS / Hybrid Frontal head restraint systems used with the helmet and harness to reduce head and neck movement in an impact. FHR use is mandatory.
Heel-and-toe Blipping the throttle while braking and downshifting so engine speed matches wheel speed, reducing rear-wheel locking and instability.
Judge of Fact An official whose judgement is treated as fact unless formally appealed with strong evidence. Timekeepers and scrutineers can be Judges of Fact.
Parc Ferme Where cars go directly after qualifying or races for eligibility checks. Stay with the car until released by the scrutineer.
Regs / regulations The sporting and technical rules. In Academy conversation, "the regs" usually means the technical rules.
Scrutineer An official checking safety or eligibility. If they find a problem, the driver has to put it right or may not be able to compete.
Testing A circuit practice day for race licence holders in competition cars. Trackday overtaking conventions do not apply.
Timekeepers / TSL Officials and timing systems that produce qualifying times, grids, race times, and live timing, usually available through TSL Timing at race events.
Track The hard surface between the track limits. Kerbs count where permitted, but going beyond the white line or far edge of the kerb can be penalised.
Setup terms Camber, caster, rake, ride height, tracking/toe, corner weights, and flat-floor setup describe how the car is adjusted to suit the driver and conditions.

Before the season

Safety Kit And Tools

The Academy package includes the car safety hardware, but the driver still needs compliant personal kit and basic tools for the season.

Personal Safety Kit

  • Mandatory: helmet, FHR, race suit, gloves, race boots, and balaclava.
  • Recommended: fireproof underwear, including top and long johns or shorts.
  • Motorcycle helmets are not permitted.
  • Helmet approvals listed in the Driver Guide include FIA 8860-2010, FIA 8859-2015, FIA 8860-2018, FIA 8860-2018-ABP, FIA 8859-2024, FIA 8859-2024-ABP, SNELL SA2015, SNELL SA2020, and SNELL SA2025.
  • SNELL SA2015 is not valid after 31 December 2026.
  • For events, pack a clear visor if you normally use a tinted visor; officials must be able to see your eyes.

Essential Tools And Supplies

  • Torque wrench capable of more than 85 Nm and a 19 mm deep socket.
  • Jack and axle stands, using the correct support points from the spanner-check page.
  • Tyre pressure gauge and tyre pump or inflator.
  • Jerry can or cans, plus a funnel or spout. Some forecourts may require permission before filling jerry cans.
  • Bucket, sponge, car shampoo, leather, and polish.
  • Engine oil, coolant, brake fluid, spare wheel, cable ties, tape, cleaning fluids, and the consumables listed on Spanner checks.

The car

Getting To Know The Car

These are the basic operating and inspection points from the Driver Guide before you start changing setup.

Starting, Running, And Instruments

  • Start with the key and red start button. Shut down with the key.
  • Do not press the red start button while the engine is running, because it engages the starter motor.
  • For the first 500 miles, avoid labouring the engine at low revs or repeatedly using very high revs.
  • The ECU rev limiter is 6600 rpm and must not be altered with an external unit.
  • Hot idle oil pressure can read in the red, but pressure should rise immediately with throttle. On track, expect around 4-5 bar.
  • If oil pressure stays in the red above 2000 rpm, check the oil immediately or speak to Caterham.

Seats, Hood, Engine Seals, And Heater

  • Choose a seat that lets the helmeted driver sit at least 50 mm below the top of the roll cage.
  • The standard race seat may need supplied washers under the front runners to change rake.
  • The gap behind the seat must be filled once the seat position is settled.
  • A custom foam or bead seat can improve comfort, support, and helmet clearance.
  • A hood is useful for paddock rain and can be used on some test days, but it is not permitted for racing.
  • Leave the three engine seals intact. If access requires removal, contact the eligibility scrutineer first.
  • The car has a heated windscreen but no heater, and a heater cannot be fitted.

Post-delivery

Preparing The Car For Competition

This is the broad preparation guidance behind the shorter Delivery prep checklist.

Driver Fit And Controls

  • Spend time adjusting pedals, seat, belts, mirrors, head restraint, steering reach, and helmet clearance before competition.
  • Adjust the clutch pedal stop so there is only a little extra travel once the clutch is fully open.
  • Set pedals so you can heel-and-toe. The brake pedal may sit closer than the throttle when static; do final pedal setup after the brakes have bedded in.
  • Helmet clearance is measured to the top of the cage, not the underside of the bars. Use a straight edge across the cage if needed.
  • If clearance is short, tilt the seat back first. If that is not enough, use a bead seat.
  • Set the head restraint as close as practical to the helmet/FHR in the normal belted-in driving position.

Bodywork, Visibility, And Tape

  • Remove wheel centre caps immediately and check every wheel set. Loose caps can hit following cars or trigger a black/orange flag report.
  • Academy cars are supplied with nylon rear wing fixings as standard. They make wing loss cleaner but can let wings detach more easily after light contact.
  • In wet conditions, use a single strip of tape between the windscreen and front cage hoop only; do not extend it further back or down the screen/cage.
  • Rain-X or similar products are commonly used on the windscreen and visor, inside and outside, but may make wipers smear.
  • The car must have three or more mirrors. Without doors, use left and right side mirrors plus a centre mirror. Sprint mirror requirements can differ because nobody is racing behind you.
  • Headlight tape is an NCR requirement to help keep broken glass together. Use tape across the glass and about 50 mm onto the headlight bowl; avoid red and blue.
  • Cover the handbrake/tunnel gap with tape or a cover so arm restraints do not catch, while keeping the handbrake functional.

Compliance, Fuel, And Data

  • Minimum weight is 635 kg including the driver in full racing kit. Drivers under about 85 kg should plan to check ballast needs.
  • Minimum ride height is 140 mm. Leave margin for tyre wear and scrutineering bay variation.
  • Race decals must be fitted to the supplied plan and kept correct through the season.
  • Race numbers must be covered, removed, or crossed out before road use.
  • Remove the front number plate before competition or track use because intercooler airflow matters.
  • The Driver Guide recommends removing the fuel inertia switch after IVA/handover using the supplied Caterham jumper, because kerbs can trip the switch and stop the engine.
  • Fit the supplied FIA roll-cage padding anywhere the helmet could contact the cage, then trim cable-tie ends safely before any wrap or finishing.
  • Torque wheel nuts to 85 Nm / 65 lb ft before every event, test session, and after working on the car. Do not over-torque them.
  • For the extinguisher, remove the green shipping seal before the first event. Keep the pin in for scrutineering, out for track sessions and Parc Ferme, and refit it when not in use.
  • Use commercially available pump fuel only. The HORSE HR13 mapping was done on 95 RON regular unleaded, so super unleaded gives no performance advantage.
  • Do not use fuel additives or illegal fuel; the Driver Guide warns that penalties can be severe.
  • Bring fuel because circuit pumps may be shut and sprint venues may not have fuel.
  • Register the MyLaps TR2 transponder before the first event and check its LED before sessions.
  • The ARDS presentation calls out a fuel sampling kit and boot floor sealing; confirm the current requirement with Caterham before the first event.

Driver safety

Harness, FHR, And Arm Restraints

The ARDS material and Driver Guide both stress that the safety equipment only works if it is fitted and worn correctly.

Harness Adjustment

  • Pre-adjust crotch, lap, and shoulder straps before events. Do not leave the first proper adjustment until the first race morning.
  • Lap straps must be very tight and worn across the pelvis, not the lower stomach.
  • Crotch straps should be firm against the legs, routed up through the seat, across the inner thigh, through the D ring on the lap strap, and back towards the buckle.
  • HANS/FHR users should have the shoulder strap adjusters on the FHR yoke area and use the inner upper harness mounting positions.
  • If shoulder straps are too long, adjust the wrap behind the seat and lock the webbing correctly.
  • Roll excess webbing and secure it so it cannot flap.

Belting-In Order

  1. Place arm restraints on your lap or passenger side ready to use.
  2. Plug in and tighten the lap straps first.
  3. Feed the crotch straps through the seat and lap-strap D rings.
  4. Put each arm-restraint loop onto the relevant shoulder-strap clasp.
  5. Pass the shoulder strap through the crotch-strap loop and plug it into the buckle.
  6. Fit the arm restraints around the wrists, just above watch position.
  7. Tighten the shoulder straps firmly. If the lap straps are tight enough, the buckle should not climb up the chest.

Arm Restraint Checks

  • Wear arm restraints on track; do not just leave them in the car.
  • Set them so fingertips can just touch the inside of the roll cage, but hands cannot pass the cage.
  • Make sure all controls and the centre mirror remain reachable.
  • The left-hand restraint is usually longer than the right. Mark the shorter right-hand strap.
  • Wear the loop on the wrist, not the forearm or upper arm.

Setup

Race Setup Basics

Setup should be done in race trim, with the driver or equivalent driver weight in the seat and tyres at hot running pressures.

Main Setup Areas

  • The Academy car must run a front anti-roll bar. The orange and red options are chosen by preference, driver style, and conditions.
  • The thicker front anti-roll bar can shift grip balance toward rear traction but may unload the inside front tyre.
  • Set front ride height no lower than 5 mm above the 140 mm minimum at the regulation reference point.
  • Set rake after front ride height. Rear ride height is measured near the front edge of the rear wing; add the chosen rake amount to the front measurement.
  • Set the right rear spring seat about 5 mm higher than the left as a starting point to compensate for driver offset.
  • Set camber after ride height. Front camber is adjusted at the top ball joint; rear camber uses shims.
  • Start caster in the middle lower-wishbone rear mounting position, then adjust with experience.
  • Set tracking last because camber changes affect toe. Toe-out is normal on the front of an Academy car.

Flat-Floor Procedure

  1. Put the car in race trim.
  2. Put the driver in race trim, or add equivalent weight.
  3. Check scale pad evenness with the driver.
  4. Remove all fuel.
  5. Weigh the car.
  6. Add ballast if required.
  7. Refill with half race-fuel capacity.
  8. Set tyre pressures.
  9. Check and adjust caster.
  10. Check and adjust ride heights.
  11. Check rake.
  12. Check and adjust camber.
  13. Recheck ride heights.
  14. Set corner weights.
  15. Check and adjust tracking.
  16. Straighten the steering wheel.
  17. Check ride heights again and repeat as needed.

Between events

Maintenance And Checks

The Driver Guide repeats the spanner-check list because regular checking is what keeps the car reliable through race weekends.

Core Maintenance Points

  • Use the Spanner checks page for torque values and physical nut-and-bolt checks.
  • Check front suspension, rear suspension, steering, engine, gearbox, wings, nose cone, lights, cooling, wheels, tyres, brake pads, and pedal feel.
  • The air filter usually needs no maintenance for the first 30,000 miles unless the car has run in dusty conditions.
  • Check oil with the engine warm, above 60 degrees C, then switched off. Wipe the dipstick, refit it, wait 20 seconds, then read it.
  • Add oil in small quantities, restart, run briefly, and recheck to avoid overfilling.
  • Keep oil at the maximum mark with the engine hot.
  • Make sure the dipstick is fully pushed home and secured so it cannot come out on track.
  • Check front wheel bearings for minimum play without being too tight.
  • Check rear brake pads regularly and inspect the inner pad carefully, as it is likely to wear first.
  • For extended track use, fit the approved Caterham competition front and rear pads listed in the regulations.
  • Keep the car clean enough that faults, leaks, loose parts, and scrutineering issues are easy to see.

Race weekends

Going Racing And Sprinting

Race weekends run to tight timetables. The recurring theme in the Driver Guide is to prepare early and arrive with the car already ready.

The Week Before

  • Clean cockpit, engine bay, suspension, wheels, and bodywork.
  • Spanner check the car and check brake pad wear.
  • Check sponsor decals, number backgrounds, race numbers, and scrutineering stickers.
  • If driving the race car to the event, cross out race numbers, remove tape from lights, inflate tyres 3-4 psi above normal pressure, and check lights.
  • If towing, check trailer tyres, wheel nuts, brakes, number plate, tie-down straps, and lights.
  • Pack the Caterham steering wheel, ignition key, and electrical cut-off key.

Documents And Kit Bag

  • Pack race licence and race upgrade card where required.
  • Pack race suit, underwear, balaclava, gloves, arm restraints, boots, helmet, and FHR.
  • Carry championship regulations and downloaded NCR Chapters 2, 5, 7, 9, and 12.
  • Carry circuit passes, final instructions, tickets, and the race-car registration document if road driving.
  • Take tools, tyre pump, pressure gauge, cleaning kit, jerry can, funnel, engine oil, coolant, brake fluid, and at least one spare wheel.

At The Event

  • Arrive early and park in the allocated paddock area.
  • Sign on as early as possible, online or in person as instructed.
  • If issued, take the scrutineering slip to the scrutineering bay.
  • For safety scrutineering, take suit, gloves, boots, helmet, and FHR as required.
  • Clean the car before scrutineering if it has been driven to the circuit.
  • Attend briefings, including first-time-driver briefings, and read any briefing notes.
  • Between scrutineering and qualifying or practice, check wheel nuts, tyre pressures, and fluid levels.
  • For qualifying, arrive at assembly early, fully dressed, belted correctly, and with arm restraints ready.
  • Complete three laps to qualify before pushing hard.
  • After each event, make notes and set a checking programme before the next meeting.

First competition

Sprint Guidance

The sprint is the first competitive step: one car at a time, against the clock, with less race traffic but the same need for preparation.

Sprint Day Notes

  • Do as much car preparation as possible before travelling; the morning will disappear quickly.
  • Read all event paperwork, supplementary regulations, and final instructions.
  • Have documents, helmet, and overalls ready for scrutineering.
  • Bring enough fuel for the day, but remember that excess fuel adds weight.
  • Drink fluids and manage energy; the day is more tiring than it looks.
  • Listen for paddock announcements and be ready for runs early.
  • At the sprint start, green means the course is clear and you may go when ready. The clock starts when you break the timing beam, so do not creep.
  • Watch your 64 ft times as a guide to start quality.
  • Watch how other drivers start and approach the course.
  • Use the sprint to compete against yourself as well as others; analyse runs and look for time.
  • Ask questions early if unsure.

First race weekend

Testing, Timetable, And Race Sessions

The first race adds testing, qualifying traffic, starts, race procedure, and Parc Ferme discipline.

Testing

  • Testing is for competition licence holders in competition cars. Trackday overtaking rules do not apply.
  • Book pre-race test days early because places are limited.
  • Use testing to learn the track, try setup changes, and practise without treating the session as a race.
  • Be aware that faster and heavier cars may overtake aggressively under braking on open test days.

Race Weekend Discipline

  • Read final instructions. Each circuit can have different procedures.
  • Make a note of all relevant times and locations. Timetables can run ahead.
  • Do not be late. Missing qualifying can mean starting at the back or not racing.
  • For self-declaration and safety checks, check rain light, brake lights, windscreen cracks, handbrake cover, clear visor, extinguisher pin procedure, transponder LED, weight, ride height, helmet clearance, and numbers.
  • Qualifying is normally 15 minutes and races are normally 20 minutes, with boards for five minutes to go and last lap.
  • Watch mirrors and stay aware of other cars at all times.
  • Under a safety car, close the field safely into a compact line. When the safety car lights go out and it pulls in, the leader controls the pace and there is no overtaking until after the start line and green signals.
  • After qualifying or racing, go directly to Parc Ferme and stay with the car until released.
  • Only drivers, scrutineers, and authorised personnel should be in Parc Ferme unless permitted.

Rules on track

Rules, Flags, Protests, And Standards

The Driver Guide highlights these because they frequently catch new drivers out.

Flags And Procedures

  • Blue flag: a faster car is approaching. Use mirrors and be predictable.
  • White flag: slow vehicle ahead.
  • Yellow flag: slow down, be prepared for danger, and do not overtake until after the green flag.
  • Yellow and red striped flag: change of surface conditions, such as oil, coolant, debris, or water.
  • Red flag: stop racing or qualifying immediately, but slow progressively, check mirrors, and signal if safe. In qualifying go to the pit lane unless instructed otherwise; in a race stop where marshals direct you and do not leave the circuit.
  • If a race is red-flagged, results or restart order may be counted back to the last completed timing point. If the red flag is on the first lap, expect the original grid to be reformed unless officials say otherwise.
  • Green flag: racing or overtaking resumes only after you pass the flag.
  • Black/white diagonal flag: driving standards warning if shown with your number.
  • Black/orange disc flag: mechanical issue; enter the pit lane and stop as instructed if shown with your number.
  • Black flag: enter the pit lane and stop as instructed if shown with your number. Do not assume a black flag is for you unless your number is displayed.
  • Chequered flag: the session is over; do not slow abruptly and do not pass it more than once.

Track Limits And Judicial Process

  • Stay on the track. A single wheel beyond the line or far side of a kerb can count as leaving the track.
  • Qualifying track-limits offences can delete the relevant lap time.
  • Race track-limits escalation in the Driver Guide is: first offence treated as misjudgement, second offence black/white flag, third offence five-second penalty, fourth offence drive-through, fifth offence exclusion.
  • If an incident needs protest, notify the Clerk within 30 minutes of results being published and follow the formal NCR process. Useful video evidence can matter.
  • If penalised, appeal through the Secretary of the Meeting within the permitted time and with the required fee. Check the current NCR for the fee amount.
  • Only the Clerk and officials can take formal action; telling Caterham personnel is not a substitute for the formal process.
  • If called to see the Clerk, use the championship handout or notes in the car to stay calm and follow the process.
  • Safe and courteous driving is the driver's responsibility. Racing is non-contact.
  • Driving Standards Improvement coaches are there to improve driving standards, not to help drivers win.
  • On trackdays, behave as an Academy ambassador. Do not use racecraft aggressively against regular trackday customers.

Driving

Qualifying, Starts, Racing, And The Tow

This section keeps the Driver Guide racecraft advice in one place for quick review before the first race weekends.

Qualifying

  • Relax, get ready early, and avoid starting the session in a panic.
  • Know the cars and drivers slightly faster than you so you can learn from them if they appear ahead.
  • Let clearly faster cars past predictably; stay on line and signal awareness rather than jumping out of the way.
  • Find space. Do not turn qualifying into a race in a group of cars.
  • A fast lap starts at the last corner of the previous lap and ends only after crossing the line.
  • Consider weather: if rain is coming, early laps may matter; if it is drying, keep going.
  • Use the whole session. The first laps are banker laps to warm car, tyres, brakes, and driver.

Race Starts And Racecraft

  • After the green-flag lap, form on the grid, watch for the five-second board and red lights, then go when the lights go out. React to the lights, not the car ahead.
  • Use enough revs for a clean launch, but aim for only a small amount of wheelspin; left-foot clutch control matters most.
  • Do not try to win the race at the first corner. You normally have enough time to create safer opportunities.
  • Overtake where you can keep speed afterwards, not just where you can briefly out-brake someone.
  • Sometimes staying second until the final tow opportunity is smarter than passing too early.
  • Pressure the car ahead by being visible in mirrors, but do not drive unpredictably or weave.
  • Defend the inside line only when needed; defensive lines are slower and can invite an outside or cutback move.
  • If you make a mistake, stay focused and keep racing. Others will also make mistakes.

The Tow And General Driving Tips

  • The Caterham creates a useful low-pressure area behind it. The following car can gain speed in the tow on long full-throttle sections.
  • To use the tow, line up behind the car ahead, stay there until close, then pull out smoothly and firmly to pass.
  • Be ready for the tow to pull you into the car ahead if you misjudge closing speed.
  • To break a tow, the lead car can move once early, then return to the racing line if needed. Weaving is not allowed.
  • Apply the brakes progressively; do not stamp on them.
  • Warm the tyres before driving quickly, especially the fronts.
  • Do not light up the rear tyres leaving the pit lane; it can create front/rear temperature imbalance and understeer.
  • Use steering inputs economically because every input costs speed.
  • At first, learn the track and your driving before blaming the car or chasing setup changes.
  • After gaining experience, experiment with tyre pressures, rake, geometry, and setup to suit your style.
  • Trackdays and tuition are useful practice, but testing and racing have different rules and traffic behaviour.
  • There is no substitute for time in the car, on road and track.

Season knowledge

Marshalling, Contacts, And Useful Reminders

The Driver Guide also covers the wider season around the car and the race weekend.

Marshalling

  • Marshals are volunteers and deserve respect and thanks. A wave at the end of sessions is appreciated.
  • After a race, whether you have won or not, acknowledge the marshals on the slowing-down lap.
  • Completing a marshalling day and getting the licence signature is worth ten championship points if done before the final event.
  • Do it early in the season if possible; it helps you understand how race meetings work from the other side.
  • Arrange marshalling through the championship contacts.

Contacts To Keep Handy

  • Championship assistants and Head of Motorsport for Academy matters.
  • Caterham Parts for permitted parts and paddock support parts.
  • Approved Caterham Motorsport Service Centres for Service Centre-only jobs.
  • HP Tyres for the mandatory Toyo Proxes CF2 tyre supply.
  • Trailer supplier contacts if using a trailer.
  • Use the latest official Driver Guide or championship communications for current names, phone numbers, and email addresses.

Mindset

  • Preparation is part of racing. Do not leave checks until the day before the next event.
  • Clean, presented cars are easier to inspect and less likely to be sent away by scrutineers.
  • Most early gains come from driver understanding, calmness, practice, and consistency rather than chasing a perfect setup.
  • To finish first, first you have to finish.
